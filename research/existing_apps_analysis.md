# Existing Apps & Games Analysis for Autism Education

## Research Objective

This report analyzes existing educational apps and games for autistic children, particularly those focused on counting, alphabet, matching, communication, and special interest integration. The goal is to identify what works, what does not, and what gaps exist that a parent-built game suite could address differently.

*Research conducted: 2026-06-28*

---

## 1. Popular Autism Apps: Mechanics & Analysis

### Endless Alphabet (Originator Inc.)

Endless Alphabet is one of the most widely recognized alphabet learning apps for children ages 3–18, including those with autism, speech delays, and dyslexia [^1].

**Core Mechanics:**
- **Letter-as-Character**: Each letter is personified as a "Monster Letter" with its own voice and personality. When tapped, the letter vocalizes its sound.
- **Drag-and-Drop Spelling**: Children drag scattered letters into a word stencil to reconstruct the word. The letters make phonetic sounds as they are dragged.
- **Definition Animations**: After completing a word, a short humorous animation plays illustrating the word's meaning, followed by a clear audio definition.
- **No Pressure Design**: No scores, timers, failures, or high-stakes feedback. Children work at their own pace [^1][^2].
- **Vocabulary Focus**: Uses advanced vocabulary words (e.g., *gargantuan*, *cooperate*, *contraption*) rather than typical "A is for apple" content, making it engaging for older learners who still need phonics support [^1].

**Limitations:** Only uppercase letters are used, which is a mismatch since 95–98% of text children encounter is lowercase [^2]. In-app purchases are required to unlock full content beyond letters A–C [^3].

### Proloquo2Go (AssistiveWare)

Proloquo2Go is the leading Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) app, widely used by nonverbal autistic children and adults [^4].

**Core Mechanics:**
- **Symbol-Based Communication**: Users communicate by tapping customizable symbols arranged on grids. Each symbol is paired with text-to-speech output.
- **Visual-Gestural Interface**: Built on the Picture Exchange Communication System (PECS) theory, allowing users to exchange visual representations for desired items or expressions [^4].
- **Extensive Customization**: Thousands of symbols available, with broad library support and voice output customization.
- **High Cost**: Premium pricing model with subscription requirements — a significant barrier for many families [^4].

### Speech Blubs (Blub Blub Inc.)

Speech Blubs is a speech therapy app specifically designed for children with autism, Down syndrome, apraxia, and speech delays [^5].

**Core Mechanics:**
- **Video Modeling**: Uses peer-to-peer video modeling instead of animation. Children watch real children model sounds and words, which research shows activates mirror neurons and facilitates imitation [^5][^6].
- **Face Filters & Voice Control**: When a child makes the correct sound, face filters (e.g., cow horns for "moo") activate, turning the child's own face into the reward.
- **Co-Play Design**: Explicitly built for adult participation alongside the child. Screen time becomes a bonding activity rather than passive consumption [^6].
- **1,500+ Activities**: Sections include "Early Sounds," "First Words," "Animal Kingdom," and "Mouth Gym" for oral motor exercises [^7].

**Criticism:** The subscription model is expensive ($9.99/month or $99.99/year), which many families find prohibitive [^7]. No ability to select specific words to target; the app determines the sequence.

### AutisMate (SpecialNeedsWare)

AutisMate combines visual schedules, scene-based learning, and video modeling into a single platform. While not as widely available as Proloquo2Go, it pioneered the concept of using the child's own environment (photos of home, school) as the learning canvas, making skills more generalizable [^4].

### ABCmouse (Age of Learning)

A comprehensive early learning curriculum for ages 2–8. While not autism-specific, its visual, step-by-step structure with rewards makes it compatible with autistic learners. However, it is a generalist platform, not tailored to sensory sensitivities or communication needs [^4].

### Autism Therapy with MITA (ImagiRation)

MITA (Mental Imagery Therapy for Autism) was recognized by Healthline as the best autism app in 2017 and is used by over 150,000 children [^8].

**Core Mechanics:**
- **Progressive Puzzle Complexity**: Teaches children to perceive multiple object characteristics simultaneously (e.g., color + shape + size), a skill linked to improved academic performance.
- **Pivotal Response Treatment (PRT)**: Grounded in PRT principles, using interactive puzzles to boost attention and language skills.
- **4-Year Curriculum**: Designed for children as young as 2, with a course lasting approximately 4 years [^8].

### Otsimo (Otsimo)

Otsimo is a comprehensive educational platform built specifically for autism, employing ABA therapy strategies [^9].

**Core Mechanics:**
- **Playful Skill Building**: Motor and cognitive skills developed through playful, game-like activities.
- **Free AAC Tools**: Includes augmentative and alternative communication tools, making it one of the few platforms that combines education and communication support.
- **Personalized Learning**: Content adapts based on the child's progress and abilities [^9].

### Autism Basics (Wellness Hub)

Autism Basics is a holistic early-intervention app designed by parents for parents, with a strong emphasis on parental involvement [^10].

**Core Mechanics:**
- **Peer Modeling Videos**: Uses slow-speed pronunciation and peer modeling (real children) to teach words.
- **Parental Control Hub**: Parents can design exercises, set duration limits, adjust difficulty levels, and control screen time in 5-minute increments.
- **8,000+ Exercises**: Covers alphabets, spellings, pre-math, math, first words, matching, body parts, memory, time, yes/no, and good manners.
- **Adaptive Difficulty**: Exercises increase in difficulty if the child is succeeding, using ABA reward principles [^10].

## 2. Counting & Math Apps for Autistic Toddlers

### Current Landscape

Math and counting apps for autistic children are generally less mature than alphabet and communication apps. Most "counting" support for autistic toddlers comes from broader educational platforms rather than dedicated apps [^11].

**What Works for Number-Loving Autistic Toddlers:**
- **Tactile Counting**: Sensory bins with countable objects (rice, beans, beads) are frequently recommended by ABA therapists for teaching early math concepts through hands-on play [^11].
- **Sorting & Categorization**: Sorting by color, size, or shape builds the foundational cognitive skills needed for math. Many apps and physical games combine sorting with counting [^11].
- **Repetitive Number Exposure**: Autistic toddlers who love numbers often benefit from repetitive, predictable counting routines — the same songs, the same sequences, the same visual presentations [^12].
- **Object-to-Number Mapping**: Using physical objects (fingers, toys, blocks) to represent numbers before abstract symbols is a common ABA technique [^12].

**Existing Digital Tools:**
- **AutiSpark Math Games**: Includes specially designed math games that are "easy to understand and play," with visual supports and progressive difficulty [^13].
- **Splash Learn**: General math games for K–Grade 5, used by some parents and therapists but not autism-specific [^14].
- **ABC Mouse**: Includes early counting and math within a broader curriculum, but with the same generalist limitations [^4].

**Identified Gap**: There is no widely recognized, autism-specific counting app that leverages the *special interest* many autistic toddlers have in numbers themselves. Most math apps treat numbers as a task to be taught rather than a passion to be channeled.

## 3. Matching & Memory Games

### Autism-Friendly Matching Game Design

Matching and memory games are foundational for autistic learners because they are highly visual, predictable, and reduce language demands [^15].

**What Existing Resources Offer:**

**Autism Little Learners (Visual Matching Activities)**
- Themed matching sets (Snowmen, Gingerbread, Masks, Halloween, Turkeys, Christmas) that include 3 interactive visual books and 8 matching activities per theme.
- Activities include: color matching, shape recognition, counting, emotion matching, job matching, and build-a-character.
- Designed for low prep: print, laminate, and add Velcro.
- Key design principles: highly visual, predictable and repetitive, short and engaging, hands-on [^15].

**AutiSpark Matching Games**
- Digital matching games for object recognition, logic development, and visual perception.
- Includes flash cards, sorting games, shadow matching, and jigsaw puzzles [^13].

**WonderTree (Free Online Games)**
- Browser-based sensory games including matching puzzles, cause-and-effect apps, and visual-motor activities.
- Designed to improve hand-eye coordination, spatial awareness, and sustained attention [^16].

**AutisticHub (Free Online Games)**
- Alphabet memory matching, words matching, shadow matching, and joining halves.
- Simple, uncluttered interfaces designed for autistic children [^17].

**Key Design Principles That Work:**
- Reduce language demands; visuals should speak for themselves.
- Provide clear expectations with consistent structure.
- Support independent work once the child learns the routine.
- Encourage success through repetition, not punishment for failure.
- Use a familiar structure so the child can focus on learning, not figuring out the rules [^15].

## 4. Special Interest Integration

### The Science of Special Interest Learning

Autistic children often develop intense, narrow interests in topics such as vehicles, dinosaurs, birds, trains, or numbers [^18]. Research shows that embedding learning within these special interests dramatically increases engagement and retention.

**The Transporters (Baron-Cohen et al.)**

One of the most validated examples is *The Transporters*, an animated series that uses vehicles with real human emotional faces to teach emotion recognition to autistic children [^19].

- **Mechanism**: Vehicles appeal to autistic children's "systemizing" affinity (predictable mechanical movement). Real human faces showing emotions are attached to the vehicles.
- **Results**: 20 children with autism (ages 4–7) watched daily for 4 weeks. Post-test scores showed significant improvement in emotion vocabulary and recognition, performing comparably to typically developing peers at Time 2 [^19].
- **Replication**: A Chinese study with 14 autistic children replicated the results, confirming cross-cultural effectiveness [^20].

**Key Insight**: When emotional learning is embedded in a highly preferred, predictable vehicle context, autistic children outperform their typical baseline because anxiety is reduced and motivation is maximized [^19].

### Common Special Interests in Autism [^18]

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Transportation | Cars, trains, buses, planes, boats |
| Animals | Dogs, birds, marine life, dinosaurs, insects |
| Technology | Computers, tablets, screensavers, geometric patterns |
| Numbers & Patterns | Counting, calendars, schedules, sequences |

**Research Note**: A 2010 study by Pierce et al. found that 40% of autistic toddlers preferred geometric patterns over social stimuli (videos of children), compared to 0% of typically developing toddlers. This suggests that apps with strong visual-geometric appeal may naturally capture attention for a significant subset of autistic children [^21].

### Existing Apps Using Special Interests

- **Dino-Sensory Exploration**: Uses dinosaur toys, play sand, and magnifying glasses for tactile exploration while teaching categorization and observation [^22].
- **Look in My Eyes Train Engineer**: Uses a train/vehicle theme to build eye contact and social interaction skills [^9].
- **Animal Kingdom (Speech Blubs)**: Channels animal special interests into speech imitation practice [^5].

**Identified Gap**: Very few apps dynamically adapt to a child's *specific* special interest. Most use a fixed theme (e.g., all vehicles or all animals). A parent-built app could allow the parent to swap in their child's current obsession — whether that is a specific bird species, a type of truck, or a particular dinosaur — and have the entire game world reflect that interest.

## 5. Gaps & Opportunities

### What Is Missing from Current Apps?

#### Gap 1: Parent-as-Designer Flexibility

No existing app allows parents to easily swap the visual assets to match their child's current special interest. If a child is obsessed with tow trucks, the parent cannot quickly make every counting object a tow truck. The closest is Autism Basics, which lets parents choose categories and set difficulty, but not customize visuals [^10].

**Opportunity**: A modular game system where parents can upload their own photos (e.g., pictures of their child's actual toy birds, favorite vehicles, or family pets) and the game engine automatically uses those assets across all activities.

#### Gap 2: Dynamic Difficulty Without Subscription Walls

Most high-quality autism apps operate on subscription models (Speech Blubs: $9.99/month; AutiSpark: $59.99/year; Proloquo2Go: premium pricing) [^7][^13]. Free alternatives (WonderTree, AutisticHub) are simpler and lack depth [^16][^17].

**Opportunity**: An open-source, free web-based suite with no paywalls. The parent controls the content, not a corporate pricing tier.

#### Gap 3: Counting Apps That Leverage Number Hyperfixation

Many autistic toddlers have an intense early interest in numbers. Existing counting apps treat numbers as abstract tasks to be learned rather than passions to be channeled. There is no autism-specific counting app that is widely recognized as a "must-have" in the way Endless Alphabet is for literacy.

**Opportunity**: A counting game designed *by* parents of a number-obsessed autistic toddler, where the mechanics are built around the joy of numbers — matching, ordering, counting, pattern recognition — rather than drilling math facts.

#### Gap 4: True Offline-First Web Apps

Most autism apps are native mobile apps requiring app stores, downloads, and accounts. Few are truly offline-first progressive web apps that work in a browser and can be installed locally without a store.

**Opportunity**: A web-based PWA that runs entirely offline, requires no login, and can be deployed on any device (tablet, laptop, phone) with a simple URL.

#### Gap 5: Integrated Parent Dashboard

While some apps (Autism Basics, Otsimo) have parent controls, they are not designed for non-technical parents to quickly see progress, adjust settings, and understand what their child is struggling with [^10][^9].

**Opportunity**: A simple, visual dashboard showing time spent per activity, success rates, and where the child gets stuck, with one-click difficulty adjustment.

#### Gap 6: Combining Multiple Skill Domains in One Suite

Existing apps tend to specialize in one domain: alphabet (Endless Alphabet), communication (Proloquo2Go), speech (Speech Blubs), math (Splash Learn), or matching (AutiSpark). Parents must download 5–10 separate apps and manage their child's attention across all of them.

**Opportunity**: A unified game suite with a consistent visual theme and interaction pattern across counting, matching, alphabet, and simple communication — reducing cognitive load for the child.

#### Gap 7: No Failure States

Even autism-friendly apps often have implicit failure (e.g., wrong answer = no star, locked level). Endless Alphabet is notable for having no failure at all [^2], but this is rare.

**Opportunity**: Design every interaction so there is no concept of "wrong" — only "try again" or "keep exploring." Rewards should be intrinsic (the bird chirps, the number glows) rather than extrinsic (stars, badges, points).

## 6. Open Source / Web-Based Projects

### Existing Open-Source Autism & AAC Projects

| Project | Description | Tech Stack | Status |
|---------|-------------|------------|--------|
| **Autsera** [^23] | Progressive web app for social skills training for high-functioning autism. Navigate places, collect stars by decoding social interactions. | React, Sass, PWA | MVP (2019), tablet-optimized |
| **Cboard** [^24] | AAC communication board using browser Speech Synthesis API. Thousands of symbols, 40 languages. | React, web-based | Active (UNICEF-backed) |
| **aac-native** [^25] | React Native PECS-based AAC app for autism. | React Native, Expo | 2017, dormant |
| **SweetSuite AAC** [^26] | Open web-based AAC app with cloud sync, supervisor roles, and offline support. | Ruby on Rails, Ember | Active (AGPLv3) |
| **OpenAAC** [^27] | Flutter app using OpenAI vector embeddings to match text to AAC symbols. Built by a parent of an autistic child. | Flutter, Pinecone, OpenAI | Active (2024) |
| **Shabd** [^28] | Android educational app for autism vocabulary. Fast word addition, pronunciation checking, progress metrics. | Android (Java) | 2019, dormant |
| **AutismX** [^29] | Flutter app for autism detection and non-verbal communication. | Flutter | 2023, limited activity |
| **FreeSpeech** [^30] | Open-source AAC app built by a sibling for his sister Della. Personalized buttons for favorite items. | Web-based | Active, community-backed |
| **Pictalk AAC** [^31] | Free AAC app with pictograms and speech synthesis. Works offline/online. | Web, iOS, Android | Active, community-funded |

### Key Technical Observations

- **AAC is the most active open-source niche**: Cboard, SweetSuite, OpenAAC, FreeSpeech, and Pictalk all focus on communication rather than educational games.
- **Educational game open-source projects are rare**: Autsera is the most relevant example of an open-source educational *game* for autism, but it focuses on social skills (reading facial expressions) rather than early academics [^23].
- **Parent-driven development is common in AAC**: OpenAAC was built by a developer father for his autistic son [^27]; FreeSpeech by a brother for his sister [^30]. This pattern validates that parents and siblings are motivated to build tools that corporations do not prioritize.
- **Web-based PWAs are underutilized**: Most open-source projects are native mobile apps (Flutter, React Native). Autsera is a notable exception as a PWA, but it is tablet-only and has not been updated since 2019 [^23].

### What a New Open-Source Project Could Learn

- From **Autsera**: Use a consistent map-based progression (places unlock as stars are earned), helper avatars for hints, and a clear visual structure.
- From **Cboard**: Browser-based deployment with text-to-speech using native Web APIs eliminates app store dependencies.
- From **OpenAAC**: Parent builders should prioritize cross-platform (Flutter/web) and offline capability.
- From **FreeSpeech**: Personalization is the killer feature. A grid of the child's *actual* favorite things is more motivating than generic symbols.

## 7. Parent Reviews: Pain Points & Wish Lists

### What Parents Say About Existing Apps

#### Positive Feedback

- **Speech Blubs**: Parents of nonverbal children praise its ability to prompt first sounds. One parent testified: "My son was diagnosed with autism a few months ago and he was non-verbal at the time. Thank you for making such an easy to use, fun, helpful app! This mama is endlessly grateful to hear her son's voice!" [^6].
- **Endless Alphabet**: Parents love that their children use advanced words in conversation (e.g., "let's CELEBRATE mommy's birthday") [^2]. The no-pressure design is repeatedly mentioned as a strength.
- **Whizkid Games**: Parents note that mainstream apps progress too quickly to advanced levels, while Whizkid is "slower pace, very easy to navigate, and really grabs his attention for a decent amount of time" [^32].
- **Autism Basics**: Parents appreciate the parental control hub and screen-time limits. The ability to "change the level of difficulty or the category anytime" is seen as essential [^10].

#### Pain Points & Frustrations

- **Cost Barriers**: Speech Blubs at $99.99/year is described as "not cheap" by a speech therapist, who acknowledges that desperate parents are "willing to spend any amount" but that the price is still prohibitive [^7].
- **Overwhelming Features**: Many apps have too many options, too many menus, or too much visual clutter. Autistic children often freeze when faced with too many choices [^23].
- **Lack of Customization**: Parents cannot easily swap in their child's special interests. One parent noted that apps with fixed themes lose their child's attention once the novelty wears off.
- **No Real Progress Insight**: While some apps track progress, parents often do not know *why* their child is struggling with a specific task or what to do next.
- **App Fatigue**: Parents report downloading 10–15 different apps and rotating between them because no single app covers all their child's needs.
- **Subscription Lock-In**: Children become attached to an app, and then the subscription expires or the device changes, disrupting the child's routine.

#### Wish Lists (Synthesized from Parent Testimonials)

1. **One app that does it all** — alphabet, counting, matching, and simple communication in a consistent visual style.
2. **Use my child's interests** — let me upload pictures of my child's actual favorite things.
3. **No recurring cost** — a free or one-time-purchase app without subscriptions.
4. **Works offline** — no dependency on internet or app stores.
5. **Works on any device** — tablet today, laptop tomorrow, parent's phone in a pinch.
6. **No failure** — my child shuts down when they get something wrong. Every interaction should be safe.
7. **Parent dashboard** — tell me what my child is doing, where they struggle, and how to adjust.
8. **Open source** — if the company goes away, the app still works. If I want to add a feature, I can.

## Gap Analysis: What the New Game Suite Should Do Differently

Based on the research above, the following principles should guide the design of a new parent-built autism educational game suite:

### 1. Unified Suite Over Specialist Apps

Instead of 5–10 separate apps, build one consistent suite where every game (counting, matching, alphabet, communication) uses the same visual language, interaction patterns, and reward system. This reduces cognitive load for the child and management burden for the parent.

### 2. Parent-Configurable Content Engine

Design the game so parents can upload their own photos and audio. If the child is obsessed with birds, every counting object, matching card, and alphabet association should be bird-themed. The parent should not need to code or use complex tools to do this — a simple file upload or drag-and-drop interface.

### 3. Zero-Failure Interaction Design

Every game mechanic should be designed so the child cannot "fail." In a matching game, a wrong match simply does nothing (or gives a gentle "not quite" sound). In a counting game, the child can tap numbers in any order and the game responds positively. In an alphabet game, letters can be explored freely without a spelling requirement.

### 4. Offline-First Progressive Web App

Build as a web app using HTML/CSS/JS that can be installed as a PWA. This eliminates app store dependencies, works on any device, and can be deployed by simply opening a URL. All assets should be cached locally for offline use.

### 5. Open Source from Day One

License the project under MIT or Apache 2.0. This ensures longevity, encourages community contribution, and aligns with the parent builder ethos seen in OpenAAC and FreeSpeech. Other parents with coding skills can contribute new game modes or language packs.

### 6. Built-In Parent Dashboard

Include a simple dashboard showing:
- Time spent per activity
- Success rates per skill
- Where the child hesitates or gets stuck
- One-click difficulty adjustment
- Ability to toggle features on/off (e.g., sound effects, animations) for sensory sensitivity

### 7. Leverage Special Interests as Motivators, Not Add-Ons

Do not treat special interests as "skins." If a child loves birds, the bird should not just be a background image — it should be the core mechanic. The bird *is* the counting object. The bird *is* the matching card. The bird's song *is* the reward sound. This is the lesson from *The Transporters*: embed the learning *inside* the special interest, not alongside it.

### 8. Prioritize Predictability Over Novelty

Autistic children thrive on repetition and predictability. The game should feel the same every time it opens, with a clear beginning and end. New content should be added gradually within the same structure, not by changing the interface. This is a key lesson from Autsera and Autism Little Learners [^23][^15].

### 9. No Subscriptions, No Ads, No Data Collection

The app should be completely free, with no in-app purchases, no advertisements, and no tracking of personal data. This aligns with parent wishes and respects the privacy of vulnerable children.

### 10. Design for the Parent Who Is Also a Developer

The parent building this app should be able to add new game modes, new themes, and new content without a complex build pipeline. A simple JSON configuration file should define the content for each game. The parent should be able to add a new "vehicle" matching game by editing a text file, not by writing code.
