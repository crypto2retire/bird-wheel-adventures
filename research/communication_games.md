## 1. Verbal Prompting Techniques in Game Design

### 1.1 Prompting Hierarchies (Least-to-Most & Most-to-Least)
Prompting hierarchies are fundamental in teaching verbal responses to autistic children. A systematic review found that **RECALL prompting** (a visual-plus-verbal hierarchy) significantly improved responsiveness in autistic children and children with language delays, increasing both overall responsiveness and meaningful correct responses [^1]. The study showed that visual prompt cards provided children with a non-linguistic avenue to respond, increasing their engagement in shared activities [^1].

**Key prompting strategies for games:**
- **Visual prompts first**: Show a picture card or symbol before providing a verbal model. Children often respond better to visual-plus-verbal prompts than verbal alone [^1].
- **Tactile prompts**: Research shows that tactile prompting devices (vibrating cues) can substantially increase verbal initiations compared to no-prompt or verbal-prompt-only conditions [^2]. A device that vibrates at regular intervals can serve as an unobtrusive cue for verbal initiation during play sessions [^2].
- **Fading prompts**: Both the prompt and reinforcement should be systematically faded over time to build independence [^2].
- **Mand-model approach**: Model the expected language response within the context of the child's interest. For example, if a child points to a toy, the adult models the word before the child receives it [^3].

### 1.2 Verbal Modeling & Echoic Prompts
In Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA), an **echoic prompt** involves verbally modeling a word or sound for the child to imitate [^4]. This is a foundational technique for early language acquisition:
- The model must be vocal, match exactly what the child should produce, occur immediately after the model, and be controlled by the auditory stimulus [^4].
- For children who do not yet echo, combining the verbal model with visual stimuli (e.g., showing a picture while saying the word, then covering it to encourage the child to say it to see it again) can spark motivation [^5].
- Research shows that when children can echo words, this skill serves as a foundation for more advanced verbal operants like **mands** (requests) and **tacts** (labels) [^4].

### 1.3 Naturalistic Prompting
Naturalistic teaching strategies embed prompts within play and daily routines rather than using isolated drills:
- **Incidental teaching**: Capitalizes on naturally occurring situations. If a child points to a toy on a screen, the game prompts the child to use a word or gesture to request it [^3].
- **Pivotal Response Training (PRT)**: Focuses on motivation and self-initiation. The child chooses the activity, and prompts are embedded within the chosen activity to increase natural engagement [^3].

## 2. Speech Therapy Gamification

### 2.1 Why Gamification Works for Speech Therapy
Gamification in Speech-Language Pathology (SLP) applies game design elements—points, levels, badges, and rewards—to therapy activities. Research shows that for children aged 3–12, the line between "learning" and "playing" is very thin, making gamification particularly effective [^6].

**Core benefits of gamified speech therapy:**
- **Increased motivation and engagement**: Children are more likely to participate actively and for longer periods when therapy activities are presented as games [^7].
- **Encouraging repetition without boredom**: Repetition is essential for muscle memory in speech sounds and language structures. Gamification wraps repetition in varied scenarios so children practice the same target multiple times without feeling like they are "doing it over and over" [^6].
- **Reduced anxiety**: Gamified therapy shifts focus from the child's challenges to the game itself, creating a more relaxed environment that leads to more natural speech production [^7].
- **Immediate feedback**: Digital games provide real-time feedback, helping children understand their progress and areas for improvement [^7].
- **Data collection**: Digital speech therapy games automatically collect detailed performance data, providing valuable insights for therapists and parents [^7].

### 2.2 Evidence-Based Gamified Interventions
A systematic review of gamified interventions for ASD found that across multiple studies, gamified designs were associated with **increased task motivation, symbolic understanding, verbal expression, and collaborative behavior** [^8]. The interventions included tablet-based language tools, augmented reality applications, and collaborative virtual environments [^8].

An AI-driven gamified intervention study with 30 children aged 4–12 (divided into pre-verbal, early vocabulary, and advanced language stages) showed that personalized game tools, with AI adjusting difficulty based on real-time performance, led to significant improvements in **participation, language skills, and social-emotional behaviors** [^9].

A serious games-based adaptive learning environment (SG-ALE) study with 14 autistic children demonstrated statistically significant enhancements in **attention and visual communication, motor and verbal imitation, receptive language, expressive language, and attention and auditory discrimination**—along with increased motivation to communicate [^10].

### 2.3 Existing Speech Therapy Game Examples
- **Speech Blubs**: Uses AI-driven speech therapy exercises with video modeling (children watch peers pronounce words) and speech recognition technology for immediate feedback. Gamified features include mini-games and face filters to sustain motivation [^11].
- **Articulation Station**: Provides structured phoneme exercises with interactive flashcards and memory games, allowing voice recording and self-assessment [^11].
- **TeachTown**: A computer-assisted intervention that teaches social and academic skills through an online curriculum using pivotal response training. Children who received TeachTown instruction showed improvement on standardized vocabulary measures, with more time in the program leading to more improvement [^12].
- **ECHOES**: A virtual agent game that supports young children on the autism spectrum in exploring and learning social communication skills, including joint attention and turn-taking [^13].
- **Hoque et al. speech therapy games**: Customized interactive games that use real-time visualizations of speech properties (e.g., a turtle race where the child controls speed by speaking slower) to improve speech intelligibility [^14].

## 3. AAC Integration in Games

### 3.1 The Role of AAC for Preschoolers with Autism
Approximately **half of those on the autism spectrum** are functionally non-verbal or minimally verbal and will not develop sufficient natural speech to meet their daily communication needs [^15]. Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) is essential for this population, and research shows that **AAC services should start at an early age**—ideally during the preschool years [^15].

### 3.2 Aided Language Modeling & Augmented Input
Research has consistently demonstrated that **augmented input** (modeling AAC use while speaking) improves both comprehension and production across spoken and augmented modalities:
- Aided language stimulation interventions reported large increases in comprehension of symbols and spoken words for preschool children with moderate intellectual disabilities [^16].
- Aided AAC modeling for preschool-aged children with autism showed increased **symbol comprehension and symbol production**, with comprehension progress often more apparent than production [^16].
- **Peer use of AAC** is associated with stronger language growth. When peers were taught to use PECS (Picture Exchange Communication System), children with autism increased their average communicative acts from 0 in baseline to 4.4 per 6-minute play session [^16].

### 3.3 Core Vocabulary vs. Fringe Vocabulary in AAC Games
The **Tell Me Program** focuses on a small set of high-frequency **core vocabulary** words (common verbs, pronouns, prepositions) that children use throughout the day, using familiar books to target these words in shared reading, writing, and classroom routines [^17].

For game design, this suggests:
- **Core words** should be prioritized: words like "look," "more," "go," "stop," "help," "want," "like" that can be used across many contexts [^17].
- **Fringe vocabulary** (specific nouns like "bee," "butterfly," "frog") can be layered on top for specific game scenes or themes [^18].
- **Visual symbol + text pairing**: Over 40% of children with ASD do not develop functional speech. AAC systems that prioritize text offer greater independence, but picture symbols are overwhelmingly used for this population. Research suggests both should be provided where possible [^19].

### 3.4 AAC Game Design Principles
A suite of evidence-based iPhone applications (SPEAKall! and SPEAKplay!) demonstrated that gamification can train the gestures needed to access mobile AAC devices:
- **SPEAKplay!** uses arcade-style games (*Pop the Bubbles*, *Fly the Rocket*, *Feed the Dino*) to train gestures including directed touch, drag-and-drop, and swiping through a gamification approach [^15].
- Usability testing with experienced AAC practitioners indicated SPEAKplay! to be a potentially viable intervention tool for learners who need both gesture training and initial speech/language skill acquisition [^15].

## 4. Echoic & Verbal Imitation Games

### 4.1 The Importance of Echoic Training
**Echoic behavior** (vocal imitation of a spoken model) is one of the earliest and most critical phases in verbal behavior for children with autism [^4]. Establishing echoic control early often leads to rapid growth in communication skills [^5]. For children who are non-speaking or minimally verbal, echoic responses help establish the foundation for all other verbal skills [^4].

Research on modeling training for echoic conversational responses found that it was effective in **improving and generalizing conversational skills** for children with ASD, with video modeling showing particular promise for increasing novel verbal responses from 2.26% in baseline to 30.43% post-training [^20].

### 4.2 Evidence-Based Imitation Game Strategies
**The Imitation Ladder** (step-by-step progression) [^21]:
1. **Gross motor imitation**: Large body movements (clapping, stomping, raising arms)
2. **Object imitation**: Copying actions with toys (rolling a car, stacking blocks)
3. **Facial/fine motor imitation**: Smiling, blowing a kiss, sticking out tongue
4. **Vocal imitation**: Simple sounds → animal sounds → environmental noises → words

**Key techniques for encouraging vocal imitation in games:**
- **Song fill-ins**: Leave off the last word of a familiar song (e.g., "Twinkle, twinkle little ___"). Children often fill in missing words during songs even if they don't echo in other contexts [^5].
- **Video modeling**: Recording an adult or peer labeling body parts, singing songs, or naming objects. One study showed a child's vocabulary jumped from 10 words to 100 words in a single session after watching targeted videos [^5].
- **Cause-and-effect apps**: Apps where the child makes a sound to trigger an effect (fireworks, bubbles popping) can motivate sound production. Emphasizing oral movements in a mirror helps [^22].
- **Combining motor + vocal**: Have the child imitate physical movements (tap head, clap hands) then quickly add verbalizing into the sequence. The task becomes "just another copy/movement" rather than a pressured speech demand [^22].
- **Contingent imitation**: After the child imitates, the adult imitates the child back, creating a reciprocal back-and-forth interaction that feels like a natural conversation [^23].

### 4.3 Imitation Games for Toddlers
- **Pat-a-cake chain**: Adult claps twice, says "Pat-a-cake!", waits, then says "Your turn." Guide hands if needed, celebrate any attempt [^21].
- **Animal pretend**: Hop like a bunny, roar like a lion—blending motor and vocal imitation. Freeze, then say "Be the animal!" [^21].
- **Mirror games**: Face the child and exaggerate movements like nose-tapping or arm raises. Add a real mirror for extra visual cues [^21].
- **Bubble play**: Blowing bubbles with "ohh" sounds, then popping. The fun leads to words [^21].
- **"Do this" with objects**: Roll a toy car, pass it over, say "Do like me." Build to two-step sequences like block stacking [^21].

## 5. Receptive vs. Expressive Language Game Mechanics

### 5.1 Understanding the Receptive-Expressive Gap
In typically developing children, receptive language (understanding) is generally stronger than expressive language (producing). However, research with preschoolers with autism shows a **serious deficit in receptive language skills relative to expressive language**, implying more difficulty in language comprehension than production [^24].

A meta-analysis of 74 studies found that overall, children with ASD showed **equally impaired receptive and expressive language skills**, both falling roughly 1.5 standard deviations below typically developing peers [^25]. However, individual children may show different profiles—some with expressive-better-than-receptive, others with the reverse [^25].

Young autistic children (12–23 months) have been found to have **significantly lower receptive language scores than expressive language scores**, with a smaller gap between receptive and expressive vocabularies than typically developing children [^26].

### 5.2 Game Mechanics for Receptive Language (Understanding)
Receptive language games should target **input**—the child's ability to understand and comprehend spoken language:
- **Following directions games**: "Touch the red ball," "Give me the cat," "Put the block in the box" [^27].
- **Picture identification**: Show an array of pictures and ask the child to point to the named item. The **Webber Functional Communication Games** include 198 receptive cards targeting matching and identification across 11 topic areas (places, playground, food, school, etc.) [^27].
- **Sorting games**: Ask the child to sort objects or picture cards into categories (animals, food, toys) while naming each item [^28].
- **Simon Says**: Simple body-part following games that combine receptive language with motor action [^29].
- **Object labeling walks**: Move through a virtual environment and label objects together; the child points or repeats [^28].
- **Interactive books**: Books with flaps, textures, or sound buttons where the child points to or touches specific items as they are named [^28].

### 5.3 Game Mechanics for Expressive Language (Producing)
Expressive language games target **output**—the child's ability to communicate thoughts and needs through words, signs, gestures, or symbols [^30]:
- **Picture naming**: Show a picture and ask "What is this?" or "Can you say apple?" [^28].
- **Sentence completion**: "The cow says ___" or "I want ___" [^27].
- **Requesting games**: Hide preferred items in a sensory bin; the child must name or request the object to get it [^29].
- **Bubble requests**: The adult holds the bubble wand; the child must say "more," "again," or "bubbles" to get more bubbles blown [^29].
- **Turn-taking dialogue**: Practice short dialogues using picture cards (e.g., "What is your name?" → "My name is ___") [^28].
- **Story building**: Take turns adding sentences to create a story, building on what the previous person said [^28].

### 5.4 Dual-Mode Games (Receptive + Expressive)
The **Webber Functional Communication Games** demonstrate an effective dual-mode design: each topic area includes both **receptive cards** (child listens and points/selects) and **expressive cards** (child produces a response) [^27]. This allows the same game structure to target both skills.

A study on digital kinetic camera games (Kinems) found that a 10-week intervention twice a week with 4–6-year-old autistic boys showed **significant improvements in both expressive and receptive vocabulary**, with gains generalizing to other activities beyond game-play sessions [^31].

## 6. Parent/Caregiver Involvement

### 6.1 Parents as Co-Interveners
Research consistently shows that **parent-mediated interventions** are highly effective for improving communication in young children with autism. The **Preschool Autism Communication Trial (PACT)** and **PLAY Project** both feature parents/carers as key agents of intervention, using play as the primary mechanism for social communication development [^32].

**Parent-Child Interaction Therapy (PCIT)**, adapted for autism, has demonstrated significant reductions in externalizing behaviors, improvements in child compliance, enhanced parent-child synchrony, and alleviated parental stress [^33].

### 6.2 Techniques for Parent-Facilitated Communication Games
Effective parent training programs teach caregivers specific techniques for encouraging back-and-forth exchanges [^34]:
- **Modeling**: Parents demonstrate reciprocal behaviors by showing how to take turns in a conversation or game.
- **Prompting and scaffolding**: Using verbal cues, gestures, or visual prompts to guide the child in initiating or responding to social exchanges.
- **Play-based learning**: Joint play activities (turn-taking games, pretend play) foster social interaction and emotional regulation.
- **Consistency and positive reinforcement**: Reinforcing reciprocal behaviors through praise, rewards, or positive attention.
- **Following the child's lead**: Using the child's interests to guide learning rather than imposing adult-directed activities [^3].

### 6.3 The PACT Approach: Video Feedback
The **PACT (Parent-mediated Paediatric Autism Communication Therapy)** approach uses video recording of parent-child playtime followed by video-feedback with therapists. This reflective approach helps parents [^35]:
- Recognize and respond to their child's communication attempts
- Adjust their language input to match the child's level
- Reduce directive/intrusive interaction patterns that can lead to child withdrawal
- Increase shared enjoyment and reciprocity during play

### 6.4 Communication-Replacement for Challenging Behaviors
Challenging behaviors (aggression, tantrums, elopement) often stem from frustration at not being able to communicate needs. ABA approaches teach parents to replace these behaviors with functional communication [^36]:
- A child who hits when wanting a toy → learns to say, sign, or point to "my turn"
- A child who screams when hungry → learns to request "eat" or show a picture of food
- A child who runs away when overwhelmed → hands a "break" card or says "all done"

### 6.5 Digital Support for Parents
Distance learning and support platforms designed to engage parents in therapeutic interaction include video courses, online consultations, chatbots, and webinars on implementing ABA methods at home [^37]. Digital platforms allow parents to:
- Track progress and structure therapy sessions
- Analyze the child's behavior patterns
- Access video modeling tailored to their child's interests [^5]
- Receive automated reports and monitor behavior over time [^37]

## 7. Web App Game Mechanic Ideas

Based on the evidence reviewed above, the following game mechanics are proposed for implementation in a web-based application targeting autistic toddlers (3–4 years) and their caregivers.

### 7.1 Echoic Training Mini-Games

| Game Mechanic | How It Works | Evidence Base |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Magic Mirror** | Child sees a video of a peer or animated character making a sound/word. The camera turns on and the child imitates. Visual feedback (e.g., stars, confetti) appears when the speech recognition engine detects a match. | Video modeling is highly effective for language acquisition [^5]; Speech Blubs uses this approach successfully [^11] |
| **Song Fill-In** | Familiar song plays (e.g., "Twinkle Twinkle Little Star") but pauses at the final word. The child must fill in the missing word. Visual support shows a picture of the missing word (star). | Song fill-ins can come before echoic control and help develop it [^5] |
| **Animal Sound Orchestra** | Tap an animal; it makes its sound. The child imitates the sound. Multiple animals can be combined into a "song." Animal sounds are often highly motivating for children with ASD [^38] | Animal sound imitation is highly effective for building speech [^38] |
| **Bubble Pop Vocal** | Bubbles float up. The child says "pop" (or makes any vocalization) to pop the bubble. The microphone detects sound and triggers the pop animation. | Cause-and-effect sound games motivate vocalization [^22] |

### 7.2 AAC-Integrated Games

| Game Mechanic | How It Works | Evidence Base |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Core Word Board** | A persistent visual board at the bottom of the screen displays 4–6 core vocabulary symbols (go, stop, more, help, want, look). Every verbal prompt in the game is paired with the symbol lighting up. The child can tap symbols to communicate at any time. | Core vocabulary approach (Tell Me Program) [^17]; aided language modeling improves comprehension and production [^16] |
| **Picture Exchange Game** | The child wants a virtual reward (e.g., a toy, sticker). They must drag the correct picture card to a "sentence strip" to request it. Starts with 1 card, progresses to 2, then 4. | PECS is effective for bridging non-verbal to verbal communication [^29] |
| **Symbol + Speech Pairing** | All spoken words in the game are simultaneously displayed as text + symbol. The child can tap any word to hear it again and see the symbol. | Augmented input improves both comprehension and production [^16] |
| **Peer AAC Avatar** | A virtual "friend" in the game uses an AAC device to communicate. The friend models AAC use during play, providing peer-mediated augmented input. | Peer use of AAC is associated with stronger language growth [^16] |

### 7.3 Receptive Language Games

| Game Mechanic | How It Works | Evidence Base |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Touch the Target** | The child hears an instruction ("Touch the red ball" / "Give the cat to the dog") and must tap or drag the correct item. 3 levels of difficulty: 2-choice, 4-choice, 9-choice array. | Webber Functional Communication Games use this structure successfully [^27]; Kinems showed significant receptive vocabulary gains [^31] |
| **Simon Says Body Parts** | An animated character gives instructions ("Touch your head," "Wave your hands"). The child follows along. The camera can optionally detect movement for confirmation. | Following directions games build receptive skills [^27]; motor imitation is the first step in the imitation ladder [^21] |
| **Where Is It?** | A scene is shown with multiple objects. The child is asked to find a specific item ("Where is the cookie?"). The item must be located in a consistent but varied location across rounds. | Object identification and picture book spotters enhance receptive vocabulary [^28] |
| **Sort the Box** | Items float by on a conveyor belt. The child hears a category ("Put the animals in the box") and must tap only the animals. | Sorting games help children understand categories and labels [^28] |

### 7.4 Expressive Language Games

| Game Mechanic | How It Works | Evidence Base |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Name It!** | A picture appears. The child is prompted "What is this?" The microphone listens for an attempt. Any vocalization is rewarded, with closer matches earning bigger rewards. | Object identification games enhance expressive labeling [^28] |
| **I Want...** | The child sees two preferred items. To get the item, they must tap the AAC symbol or speak the word. The game uses a "sabotage" technique where the item is briefly hidden, encouraging the child to request it. | Naturalistic teaching uses child interest and environmental arrangement to prompt requests [^3] |
| **Finish the Story** | A simple 3-panel story is shown. The narrator says the first two panels and pauses. The child must select or say the final panel/word to complete the story. | Story sequencing and completion build expressive narrative skills [^28] |
| **My Turn, Your Turn** | A virtual ball is passed back and forth. The game says "My turn" (adult/character plays) then "Your turn" (child must say "my turn" or tap the symbol to receive the ball). | Turn-taking is fundamental to conversational flow [^29] |

### 7.5 Parent-Child Co-Play Mode

| Game Mechanic | How It Works | Evidence Base |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Dual Screen / Pass-and-Play** | The game is designed for parent and child to sit together. Some rounds require the parent to hold the device while the child responds, and vice versa. Prompts appear for the parent (e.g., "Say 'your turn' and hand the tablet to your child"). | Parent-child interaction therapy shows enhanced synchrony and reduced stress [^33]; parent modeling is a key technique [^34] |
| **Parent Prompt Coach** | A "coach panel" on the parent side of the screen suggests what to say, how to prompt, and when to fade help. For example: "Now cover the picture and ask your child to say the word." | Covering pictures to encourage verbalization is a known trick for establishing echoic control [^5] |
| **Video Reflection** | The app records 10-second clips of parent-child play. After the session, the parent can watch a highlight reel with positive moments tagged ("Here your child imitated you!"). | PACT uses video feedback for parent reflection [^35] |
| **Home Challenge** | After completing a digital game, the app suggests a real-world activity to try (e.g., "Practice 'more' during snack time today"). Parents can log completion. | Embedding learning in daily routines promotes generalization [^3] |

### 7.6 Prompting & Scaffolding System

| Feature | How It Works | Evidence Base |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| **Adaptive Prompt Fading** | The game starts with full visual + verbal prompts (picture shown + word spoken). As the child succeeds, it fades to verbal-only, then to a partial verbal cue, then to independence. | Prompt fading is essential for building independence [^2] |
| **Wait Time Setting** | Adjustable pause after a prompt (3, 5, 10 seconds) before the next prompt level appears. Parents can set the wait time based on their child's processing speed. | Allowing waiting time before expecting a response is a recommended support strategy [^24] |
| **Tactile Reminder** | If the child has a wearable or the device has haptic feedback, a gentle vibration can cue the child to "try saying something" when they've been silent for a set period. | Tactile prompting increased verbal initiations substantially compared to verbal-only prompts [^2] |
| **Least-to-Most Prompting** | The game automatically escalates prompts: (1) expectant pause, (2) visual cue, (3) verbal model, (4) partial verbal model, (5) physical guidance (if applicable). | RECALL prompting hierarchy improved responsiveness [^1] |

### 7.7 Gamification & Motivation Layer

| Feature | How It Works | Evidence Base |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| **Sticker Album** | Every successful communication attempt earns a virtual sticker. The sticker album is organized by category (sounds, words, requests, turn-taking). | Tangible rewards and visual progress tracking motivate children with ASD [^7] |
| **Level Progression** | The child "levels up" by mastering skills (e.g., Level 1: 5 gross motor imitations; Level 2: 10 vocal imitations; Level 3: 3-word requests). AI adjusts difficulty based on real-time performance. | AI-driven adaptive difficulty showed significant improvements in participation and language [^9] |
| **Character Customization** | The child earns tokens to dress or customize a virtual companion character. The character responds to the child's communication attempts, providing social reinforcement. | Custom avatars and virtual worlds increase engagement in SLP gamification [^6] |
| **Session Timer with Transition Warning** | A visual timer shows how much game time is left. A 2-minute warning appears to help the child prepare for the end of the session. | Clear transitions and visual schedules reduce frustration [^29] |
| **First-Then Board** | A visual schedule within the game: "First do 3 speech games, then get free play time." Helps the child understand the session structure. | Visual schedules help children with autism thrive with structure and predictability [^29] |
